  • Resumé

    • I am a PhD candidate in the Computer Science Department at Stanford University studying databases and computer architecture. I received a Masters degree in Electrical Engineering from Stanford University (June 2015) and a Bachelor of Science in Computer Engineering and Computer Science from the University of Wisconsin-Madison (Go Badgers!).

    I study the intersection of cutting edge database theory and modern hardware trends-in particular high performance join processing. I conduct research under Professors Christopher Ré and Kunle Olukotun. I am a member of Hazy Research, the InfoLab, and the Pervasive Parallelism Laboratory.

    Did you know that the bottleneck operation in traditional databases uses an algorithm with a suboptimal asymptotic bound? We do! Using recent theoretical guarantees and modern hardware advances we have developed a new style of relational data processing engine that provides tight theoretical guarantees. Our engine consists of a novel query compiler and storage engine. Our query compiler uses a new intermediate representation (replacing the role of relational algebra) for query plans that provides strong asymptotic guarantees. Our storage engine which is designed for the single-instruction multiple data (SIMD) hardware trend. We have applied our work to the graph and RDF domains but we are quickly expanding out to other workloads. We demonstrate high performance when compared to traditional data processing engines and competitive performance when compared to specialized graph and RDF engines. My previous research has focused on designing a graph analytics DSL in an advanced compiler framework for parallel and heterogeneous architectures.

    Relevant technical course work includes: databases, algorithms, computer architecture, operating systems, machine learning, programming languages, computer graphics, digital system design and synthesis, and logic design.
